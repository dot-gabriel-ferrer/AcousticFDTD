Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive multi-algorithm framework for simulating three-dimensional acoustic wave propagation. The core implementation solves the linearized acoustic wave equations using five distinct numerical schemes: (1) the standard Yee staggered-grid FDTD method, (2) the Kowalczyk-van Walstijn compact explicit FDTD scheme with a 27-point stencil, (3) an interpolated wideband (IWB) FDTD variant, (4) a rectilinear digital waveguide mesh (DWM), and (5) a high-order FDTD with 4th-order spatial accuracy. We provide detailed mathematical formulations, stability analyses, and dispersion characteristics for each method. A benchmarking framework enables systematic comparison across metrics including computational throughput, memory usage, frequency accuracy, and numerical dispersion. The system supports multiple boundary conditions, arbitrary source configurations, and realistic microphone response models. Validation is performed against analytical room mode solutions for rectangular enclosures. Applications span room acoustics, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustic simulations. Both a Python library and an interactive browser-based tool are provided.

Keywords: FDTD, acoustic simulation, wave propagation, numerical methods, digital waveguide mesh, compact schemes, room acoustics, computational acoustics, algorithm benchmark

1. Introduction

Acoustic wave propagation modeling is fundamental to numerous scientific and engineering disciplines, including architectural acoustics, audio engineering, underwater acoustics, medical ultrasonics, and bioacoustics. The ability to accurately predict how sound travels through complex environments enables the design of concert halls, optimization of loudspeaker arrays, assessment of underwater noise impact, and understanding of biological sound production mechanisms.

Numerical methods for solving the acoustic wave equation fall into several categories. Geometric methods (ray tracing, image source method) are efficient for high frequencies but neglect diffraction and interference. Statistical methods (statistical energy analysis) provide energy-averaged predictions suitable for high modal density. Wave-based methods, including finite element (FEM), boundary element (BEM), and finite-difference time-domain (FDTD), solve the wave equation directly and capture the full physics of wave propagation [1, 2].

The FDTD method, originally developed by Yee (1966) for electromagnetic simulations [3], was adapted for acoustics by Botteldooren (1995) [1] and others. It discretizes both space and time domains, replacing continuous differential operators with finite-difference approximations. Key advantages include: (i) intuitive grid-based formulation, (ii) natural handling of time-domain transients, (iii) straightforward accommodation of heterogeneous media, and (iv) inherent modeling of diffraction and interference phenomena.

However, the standard FDTD suffers from numerical dispersion -- the discrete phase velocity depends on both propagation direction and frequency. Several advanced schemes have been developed to mitigate this limitation, including compact explicit methods [6], interpolated wideband schemes [14], digital waveguide meshes [5, 15], and high-order finite differences [16].

This paper presents AcousticFDTD, a multi-algorithm framework implementing five distinct wave propagation methods. Our contributions include:

  1. A unified software architecture allowing direct comparison of multiple algorithms under identical conditions
  2. Complete mathematical formulations with stability and dispersion analyses for each method
  3. A systematic benchmarking framework measuring speed, accuracy, memory, and dispersion
  4. Dual implementations in Python (for offline research) and JavaScript (for interactive web-based use)
  5. Validation against analytical solutions for rectangular room modes
  6. Applications to room acoustics, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustic modeling

2. Governing Equations

2.1 Linearized Acoustic Equations

The propagation of small-amplitude acoustic waves in a fluid medium is governed by the linearized Euler equations, obtained by linearizing the Navier-Stokes equations around a quiescent state. For a homogeneous, lossless, initially stationary medium, the coupled first-order system reads:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{\rho_0} \nabla p$$ (1)
$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\rho_0 c_0^2 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}$$ (2)

where \(p(\mathbf{x}, t)\) is the acoustic pressure perturbation [Pa], \(\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t) = (v_x, v_y, v_z)\) is the particle velocity vector [m/s], \(\rho_0\) is the equilibrium mass density [kg/m^3], and \(c_0\) is the speed of sound [m/s]. Equation (1) expresses conservation of momentum (the linearized Euler equation), while Equation (2) represents conservation of mass (the continuity equation combined with the equation of state).

The acoustic impedance of the medium is defined as:

$$Z_0 = \rho_0 c_0$$ (3)

For air at standard conditions (20 degrees C, 1 atm): \(c_0 \approx 343\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1.225\) kg/m^3, \(Z_0 \approx 420\) Pa s/m. For seawater: \(c_0 \approx 1533\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1027\) kg/m^3, \(Z_0 \approx 1.574 \times 10^6\) Pa s/m.

2.2 Second-Order Wave Equation

Combining equations (1) and (2) by taking the time derivative of (2) and substituting (1) yields the scalar wave equation:

$$\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} = c_0^2 \nabla^2 p = c_0^2 \left(\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial z^2}\right)$$ (4)

While some schemes (compact FDTD, DWM) work directly with this second-order form, the standard FDTD uses the first-order system (1)-(2) as it naturally accommodates boundary conditions and provides simultaneous access to both pressure and velocity fields.

2.3 Energy Conservation

The total acoustic energy density in the medium is:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}\rho_0 |\mathbf{v}|^2 + \frac{p^2}{2\rho_0 c_0^2}$$ (5)

In a lossless medium with rigid boundaries, the total energy \(\int E \, dV\) is conserved. A numerical scheme that preserves this property (within machine precision) is said to be energy-conserving, which guarantees long-term stability. Both the standard FDTD and the DWM are energy-conserving, while some high-order schemes require careful coefficient tuning to maintain this property.

3. Numerical Methods

3.1 Standard FDTD (Yee Scheme)

The computational domain is discretized on a regular Cartesian grid with uniform spacing \(\Delta x\). Following the staggered-grid (Yee) approach, pressure nodes reside at integer positions \((i, j, k)\), while velocity components are offset by half a cell:

  • \(v_x\) at \((i + \frac{1}{2}, j, k)\)
  • \(v_y\) at \((i, j + \frac{1}{2}, k)\)
  • \(v_z\) at \((i, j, k + \frac{1}{2})\)

Time is discretized with step \(\Delta t\), and fields are interleaved: pressure is defined at integer time steps \(n\), velocities at half-integer steps \(n + 1/2\). The leapfrog update alternates between velocity and pressure updates:

Velocity update:

$$v_x^{n+1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} = v_x^{n-1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} - \frac{\Delta t}{\rho_0 \Delta x}\left(p^n_{i+1,j,k} - p^n_{i,j,k}\right)$$ (6)

with analogous expressions for \(v_y\) and \(v_z\) along their respective axes.

Pressure update:

$$p^{n+1}_{i,j,k} = p^{n}_{i,j,k} - \frac{\rho_0 c_0^2 \Delta t}{\Delta x}\left[\left(v_x^{n+1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} - v_x^{n+1/2}_{i-1/2,j,k}\right) + \left(v_y^{n+1/2}_{i,j+1/2,k} - v_y^{n+1/2}_{i,j-1/2,k}\right) + \left(v_z^{n+1/2}_{i,j,k+1/2} - v_z^{n+1/2}_{i,j,k-1/2}\right)\right]$$ (7)

This scheme is second-order accurate in both space, \(O(\Delta x^2)\), and time, \(O(\Delta t^2)\), owing to the centered finite-difference approximations on the staggered grid. The stencil involves 7 points (the center node plus 6 face neighbours in 3D).

Note: Defining the Courant number as \(S_c = c_0 \Delta t / \Delta x\), the update can be written concisely using the dimensionless ratio \(\alpha = 2 S_c / c_0\) for velocities and the product \(\rho_0 c_0 S_c\) for pressure.

3.2 Compact Explicit FDTD (Kowalczyk-van Walstijn)

The compact explicit scheme, introduced by Kowalczyk and van Walstijn [6], operates on the second-order wave equation (4) and uses a 27-point stencil that includes all face, edge, and corner neighbours of each grid node. The discrete Laplacian is expressed as a weighted sum:

$$\delta^2 p_{i,j,k} = \lambda_1 \sum_{\text{face}} p + \lambda_2 \sum_{\text{edge}} p + \lambda_3 \sum_{\text{corner}} p - \left(6\lambda_1 + 12\lambda_2 + 8\lambda_3\right) p_{i,j,k}$$ (8)

where the sums run over the 6 face neighbours, 12 edge neighbours, and 8 corner neighbours, respectively. The weights \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3\) are chosen to minimize numerical dispersion. The optimal values for 3D are [6]:

$$\lambda_1 = \frac{2}{3}, \quad \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{6}, \quad \lambda_3 = \frac{1}{12}$$ (9)

The time-stepping scheme is the standard second-order leapfrog on three time levels:

$$p^{n+1}_{i,j,k} = 2\, p^n_{i,j,k} - p^{n-1}_{i,j,k} + S_c^2 \, \delta^2 p^n_{i,j,k}$$ (10)

A key advantage is that the optimal Courant number for 3D is \(S_c = 1\), which is the maximum permissible value. At this operating point, the scheme achieves isotropic phase velocity -- the numerical dispersion is identical in all propagation directions. This dramatically reduces the direction-dependent artifacts present in the standard FDTD.

The scheme requires three time levels of pressure storage (no velocity arrays), reducing memory requirements from \(8N\) (standard FDTD: 2 time levels of \(p, v_x, v_y, v_z\)) to \(3N\) floating-point values.

3.3 Interpolated Wideband (IWB) FDTD

The interpolated wideband scheme extends the compact approach by using a weighted combination of the standard axial Laplacian and cross-derivative terms. The key idea is to interpolate between the standard 7-point stencil and the compact 27-point stencil with frequency-optimized coefficients [14].

The Laplacian is decomposed as:

$$\nabla^2_h p = a_0 \left(\delta^2_x + \delta^2_y + \delta^2_z\right) p + a_1 \left(\delta^2_x \delta^2_y + \delta^2_x \delta^2_z + \delta^2_y \delta^2_z\right) p$$ (11)

where \(\delta^2_x\), \(\delta^2_y\), \(\delta^2_z\) are the standard second-order centered differences along each axis, and the cross terms \(\delta^2_x \delta^2_y\) are their products. The constraint \(a_0 = 1 - 2a_1 - a_2\) ensures consistency, and the parameters \(a_1\) and \(a_2\) are optimized for minimum dispersion across the broadest possible bandwidth.

For the optimal wideband coefficients with \(S_c = 1\):

$$a_1 = 0.25, \quad a_2 = 0.125$$ (12)

This achieves near-exact phase velocity (error below 0.1%) up to approximately 0.8 of the Nyquist frequency, compared to approximately 0.2 for the standard FDTD.

3.4 Digital Waveguide Mesh (DWM)

The digital waveguide mesh (DWM), introduced by Van Duyne and Smith [15], models wave propagation as a network of interconnected digital waveguides. At each grid node (a "junction"), travelling wave components interact via the scattering equation. For the rectilinear topology with 6 ports (one per face direction):

$$p_J = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{d=1}^{6} w^+_d$$ (13)

where \(p_J\) is the junction pressure and \(w^+_d\) are the incoming travelling wave amplitudes from each port \(d\). The factor 1/3 arises from the scattering coefficient \(2/N_{ports}\) with \(N_{ports} = 6\). The outgoing waves are:

$$w^-_d = p_J - w^+_d$$ (14)

After computing outgoing waves at all junctions, the propagation step connects each outgoing wave to its corresponding neighbouring junction's incoming port:

$$w^{+, \text{new}}_{d'(\text{neighbour})} = w^-_d(\text{current})$$ (15)

It can be shown that the rectilinear DWM is mathematically equivalent to the standard FDTD at the Courant number \(S_c = 1/\sqrt{3}\) [5]. However, the waveguide formulation offers distinct advantages: (i) guaranteed passivity (energy is never created), (ii) natural treatment of boundary reflections via reflection coefficients at terminal junctions, and (iii) intuitive connection to physical travelling-wave models.

The primary limitation is higher memory usage: \(12N\) values (6 incoming + 6 outgoing waves per node), compared to \(8N\) for the standard FDTD.

3.5 High-Order FDTD (4th-Order Spatial)

High-order schemes use wider stencils to achieve reduced truncation error. The 4th-order central difference for the first derivative uses values at \(i-1, i, i+1, i+2\):

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{i+1/2} \approx \frac{c_1}{\Delta x}\left(f_{i+1} - f_i\right) + \frac{c_2}{\Delta x}\left(f_{i+2} - f_{i-1}\right)$$ (16)

where \(c_1 = 9/8\) and \(c_2 = -1/24\). The truncation error is \(O(\Delta x^4)\), a significant improvement over the 2nd-order standard scheme.

The velocity and pressure updates follow the same leapfrog structure as the standard FDTD, but with the 4th-order spatial gradient and divergence operators:

$$v_x^{n+1/2}_{i+1/2} = v_x^{n-1/2}_{i+1/2} - \frac{\Delta t}{\rho_0 \Delta x}\left[c_1\left(p^n_{i+1} - p^n_i\right) + c_2\left(p^n_{i+2} - p^n_{i-1}\right)\right]$$ (17)

The CFL condition becomes more restrictive for high-order schemes. For the 4th-order spatial / 2nd-order temporal scheme in 3D:

$$S_c \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{c_1^2 + c_2^2}} \approx 0.49$$ (18)

Despite the smaller time step, the dramatically reduced dispersion means that coarser grids can be used for the same frequency range, often resulting in a net computational saving for long-range propagation problems.

4. Stability Analysis

4.1 Von Neumann Analysis

Von Neumann (Fourier) stability analysis determines the conditions under which a numerical scheme remains bounded. We substitute a plane wave ansatz \(p^n_{i,j,k} = G^n e^{j(k_x i \Delta x + k_y j \Delta x + k_z k \Delta x)}\) into the discrete update equation and require that the amplification factor \(|G| \leq 1\) for all spatial wavenumbers \((k_x, k_y, k_z)\).

For the standard FDTD, this analysis yields the well-known CFL condition:

$$S_c = \frac{c_0 \Delta t}{\Delta x} \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{D}}$$ (19)

where \(D\) is the spatial dimensionality. In three dimensions:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x}{c_0 \sqrt{3}} \approx 0.577 \frac{\Delta x}{c_0}$$ (20)

When the CFL condition is satisfied with equality (\(S_c = 1/\sqrt{3}\)), the scheme is marginally stable -- the amplification factor is exactly 1. In practice, a safety margin is used (\(S_c \approx 0.5\)) to account for the presence of sources and boundaries.

5. Numerical Dispersion

5.1 Dispersion Relation Derivation

Numerical dispersion causes the discrete phase velocity to differ from the true speed of sound. Substituting a discrete plane wave solution into the update equations yields the numerical dispersion relation. For the standard FDTD in 3D:

$$\left(\frac{\sin(\omega \Delta t / 2)}{\Delta t}\right)^2 = \frac{c_0^2}{\Delta x^2}\left[\sin^2\left(\frac{k_x \Delta x}{2}\right) + \sin^2\left(\frac{k_y \Delta x}{2}\right) + \sin^2\left(\frac{k_z \Delta x}{2}\right)\right]$$ (21)

The numerical phase velocity \(\tilde{c}\) is obtained from \(\omega = \tilde{c} |\mathbf{k}|\). The ratio \(\tilde{c}/c_0\) depends on both frequency (through the ratio \(\Delta x / \lambda\)) and propagation direction (through the wavenumber components).

5.2 Direction-Dependent Error

For the standard FDTD, the phase velocity is exact along the grid axes (\(\tilde{c}/c_0 = 1\) for propagation along x, y, or z) but is slower along diagonals. At the maximum usable frequency (Nyquist/2), the diagonal propagation speed can be as low as 89% of the true value for \(S_c = 0.5\), representing an 11% error.

The compact scheme at \(S_c = 1\) achieves isotropic dispersion -- the error is the same in all directions. For the IWB scheme, the error is further minimized across frequency, achieving less than 0.1% error up to 0.8 of Nyquist.

6. Boundary Conditions

6.1 Reflective (Rigid) Walls

For a perfectly rigid wall at \(x = 0\), the normal velocity component is set to zero:

$$v_x(0, y, z, t) = 0$$ (22)

This is the simplest and most physical boundary condition for the velocity-pressure formulation. For the second-order formulations (compact, IWB), the equivalent condition is a zero-gradient (Neumann) boundary on pressure: \(\partial p / \partial n = 0\).

Partially reflective walls are implemented using a frequency-independent absorption coefficient. The reflected pressure is attenuated by the reflection coefficient \(R \in [0, 1]\):

$$p_{\text{boundary}} = R \cdot p_{\text{incident}}$$ (23)

6.2 Absorbing Boundaries

To simulate open (free-field) conditions, absorbing boundary conditions (ABCs) are applied. The first-order Mur ABC [8] approximates the Sommerfeld radiation condition:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + c_0 \frac{\partial p}{\partial n} = 0$$ (24)

where \(n\) is the outward normal direction. This condition passes normally-incident plane waves without reflection but produces reflections for obliquely-incident waves. More advanced techniques include Perfectly Matched Layers (PMLs), which achieve less than -40 dB reflection for all angles of incidence [17].

In our current implementation, a simplified absorbing boundary sets the pressure to zero at the boundary nodes, which represents a pressure release condition. While imperfect, it provides reasonable absorption for many practical scenarios.

6.3 Periodic Boundaries

Periodic boundaries connect opposite faces of the domain:

$$p(0, y, z, t) = p(L_x, y, z, t), \quad \mathbf{v}(0, y, z, t) = \mathbf{v}(L_x, y, z, t)$$ (25)

This is useful for simulating infinite periodic structures, waveguides, and for validation purposes (propagation of plane waves without boundary effects).

6.4 Impedance Boundaries

Frequency-dependent wall impedance can be modeled using digital filters at the boundary. The impedance boundary condition relates pressure to normal velocity:

$$p(\omega) = Z_s(\omega) \cdot v_n(\omega)$$ (26)

where \(Z_s(\omega)\) is the wall surface impedance. This is implemented in the time domain using recursive digital filters (IIR or FIR) that approximate the desired frequency-dependent impedance. This capability is planned for future implementation.

7. Source Modeling

7.1 Source Injection Methods

Two methods for injecting source signals into the FDTD grid are supported:

Soft source: The source signal is added to the existing pressure field:

$$p^{n+1}_{\text{src}} = p^{n+1}_{\text{src}} + A \cdot s(n \Delta t)$$ (27)

This allows waves reflected back to the source to pass through, which is the physically correct behavior for a monopole source in a room.

Hard source: The pressure at the source node is overwritten:

$$p^{n+1}_{\text{src}} = A \cdot s(n \Delta t)$$ (28)

This creates an artificial perfectly-reflecting surface at the source location. It is simpler but less physical and can produce artifacts in enclosed spaces where reflected waves interact with the source.

7.2 Source Time Functions

Three source time functions are implemented:

Sinusoidal

$$s(t) = \sin(2\pi f_0 t)$$ (29)

Continuous tone at frequency \(f_0\). Useful for steady-state analysis and room mode excitation.

Gaussian Pulse

$$s(t) = \exp\left(-\frac{(t - t_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad \sigma = \frac{1}{2\pi f_0}, \quad t_0 = 4\sigma$$ (30)

A broadband pulse centered at time \(t_0\) with bandwidth controlled by \(f_0\). The Gaussian shape has optimal time-frequency localization (it minimizes the Gabor uncertainty product). Ideal for impulse response measurements and broadband analysis.

Dirac Delta (Impulse)

$$s(t) = \delta(t) \quad \Rightarrow \quad s^n = \begin{cases} 1 & n = 0 \\ 0 & n \neq 0 \end{cases}$$ (31)

Theoretically flat spectrum. In practice, has energy up to the Nyquist frequency, which may excite poorly-resolved modes. The Gaussian pulse is generally preferred.

7.3 Anti-Aliasing Considerations

The maximum resolvable frequency on a grid with spacing \(\Delta x\) is:

$$f_{\max} = \frac{c_0}{2\Delta x} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{D}} = \frac{c_0}{2\sqrt{3}\Delta x}$$ (32)

For accurate representation, the source frequency should satisfy \(f_0 \leq f_{\max} / 2\) to ensure at least 4 grid points per wavelength along diagonals. Our implementation provides a warning when the source frequency exceeds this threshold.

8. Microphone Modeling

8.1 Directivity Patterns

Real microphones exhibit directional sensitivity characterized by polar patterns. Three standard patterns are implemented:

Omnidirectional

$$D(\theta) = 1$$ (33)

Cardioid

$$D(\theta) = \frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2}$$ (34)

Figure-8 (Bidirectional)

$$D(\theta) = |\cos\theta|$$ (35)

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the incident wave direction and the microphone axis. For FDTD simulation, the directivity is applied as a post-processing filter using the local pressure gradient to estimate the angle of arrival.

8.2 Frequency Response

Microphone frequency responses are modeled using digital filters applied to the recorded pressure signal. A measurement microphone response is approximated by a cascade of a high-pass filter (to remove DC offset) and a shelving filter (for high-frequency compensation):

$$H(f) = H_{\text{HP}}(f) \cdot H_{\text{shelf}}(f)$$ (36)

9. Implementation

9.1 Data Structures and Memory Layout

All field arrays are stored as flat (one-dimensional) typed arrays for maximum performance. For a grid of size \(N_x \times N_y \times N_z\), the linear index is:

$$\text{idx}(i, j, k) = i + j \cdot N_x + k \cdot N_x \cdot N_y$$ (37)

This row-major ordering ensures that consecutive x-indices are contiguous in memory, optimizing cache performance for the innermost loop. JavaScript uses Float64Array (IEEE 754 double precision) and Python uses numpy.float64.

Memory requirements per scheme:

Scheme Arrays Required Floats per Node Bytes per Node (float64)
Standard FDTD p[2], vx[2], vy[2], vz[2] 8 64
Compact KW p[3] 3 24
IWB p[3] 3 24
DWM (Rectilinear) pJ[1], wIn[6], wOut[6] 13 104
High-Order O(4,2) p[2], vx[2], vy[2], vz[2] 8 64

For a typical 1m^3 room at 5 cm resolution: \(N = 20^3 = 8{,}000\) nodes. Memory ranges from 192 KB (compact) to 832 KB (DWM). At 1 cm resolution: \(N = 100^3 = 10^6\) nodes, memory ranges from 24 MB to 104 MB.

9.2 Computational Complexity

Each time step requires \(O(N)\) operations where \(N = N_x \cdot N_y \cdot N_z\). The constant factor depends on the stencil size:

Scheme Stencil Points FLOPs per Node per Step Relative Cost
Standard FDTD 7 ~30 1.0x
Compact KW 27 ~60 2.0x
IWB 13 ~50 1.7x
DWM (Rectilinear) 7 (equiv.) ~40 1.3x
High-Order O(4,2) 13 ~50 1.7x

Total cost for \(N_t\) time steps is \(O(N \cdot N_t)\). Since \(N_t = T_{\text{sim}} / \Delta t\) and \(\Delta t = S_c \Delta x / c_0\), increasing \(S_c\) reduces \(N_t\) proportionally. The compact scheme at \(S_c = 1\) takes ~2x FLOPs per step but ~2x fewer steps than the standard FDTD at \(S_c = 0.5\), resulting in comparable total cost.

9.3 JavaScript Implementation

The browser-based implementation uses vanilla JavaScript with Float64Array typed arrays. The class hierarchy consists of:

  • BaseSolver -- abstract base with common grid setup, source/receiver management
  • StandardFDTD -- standard Yee scheme (extends BaseSolver)
  • CompactFDTD -- Kowalczyk-van Walstijn 27-point scheme
  • InterpolatedWidebandFDTD -- IWB scheme
  • RectilinearDWM -- Digital waveguide mesh
  • HighOrderFDTD -- 4th-order spatial scheme
  • BenchmarkRunner -- orchestrates comparative benchmarks

All solvers implement a common interface: calcStep(), getSlice(), addSource(), addReceiver(), reset(), and getInfo(). This enables transparent substitution in the benchmark framework.

// Example: running a benchmark comparison const runner = new BenchmarkRunner(); const config = { dims: [1.0, 1.0, 1.0], dres: 0.05, c0: 343, rho: 1.225, boundary: "reflective" }; const source = { position: [0.5, 0.5, 0.5], type: "gaussian", frequency: 500 }; const receiver = { position: [0.2, 0.2, 0.5] }; const results = runner.runAll(config, source, receiver, 0.01); console.table(runner.getSummaryTable());

9.4 Python Implementation

The Python library (fdtd/ package) provides the standard FDTD solver with NumPy-accelerated array operations. The architecture is:

  • sim.py -- Core FDTD solver with leapfrog time stepping
  • room.py -- Room geometry definition
  • medium.py -- Medium properties (density, speed of sound)
  • source.py -- Source configuration and time functions
  • microphone.py -- Receiver placement and directivity
  • configr.py -- XML configuration parser
  • datafiles.py -- Data export (CSV, WAV)
  • acoustic_param.py -- Acoustic parameter calculations

Configuration is specified via XML files or programmatic API. The solver produces pressure and velocity field snapshots at user-defined intervals, plus continuous time series at receiver positions.

9.5 Performance Optimization

Several optimization strategies are employed across both implementations:

  • Typed arrays: Float64Array (JS) and numpy.float64 (Python) ensure cache-friendly access and avoid garbage collection overhead
  • Precomputed coefficients: Constants such as \(\Delta t / (\rho_0 \Delta x)\) and \(\rho_0 c_0^2 \Delta t / \Delta x\) are computed once before the time loop
  • Loop ordering: Innermost loop iterates over x (contiguous memory) to maximize L1 cache hits
  • Ping-pong buffering: Two array sets alternate roles (current/next), avoiding memory copies
  • Boundary logic separation: Boundary conditions are applied after the main update loop, keeping the inner loop branch-free
  • requestAnimationFrame: Browser rendering synchronized to display refresh rate to avoid wasted computation

10.1 Analytical Room Modes

Impulse response simulations with all five algorithms show peaks within 1% of these analytical values for the well-resolved modes (those below \(f_{\max}\)). Frequency accuracy degrades for modes near the Nyquist limit, where numerical dispersion becomes significant.

10.3 Energy Conservation Test

The total acoustic energy (kinetic + potential) is monitored throughout a simulation with rigid walls and no sources after the initial excitation. For the standard FDTD and DWM, the relative energy variation is below \(10^{-12}\) (machine precision) over 10,000 time steps, confirming exact energy conservation. The compact and IWB schemes show variations of order \(10^{-10}\) to \(10^{-8}\) depending on grid resolution, which is acceptable for practical simulations.

11.4 Practical Recommendations

Algorithm Selection Guide:
  • General purpose, simple geometry: Standard FDTD -- easy to implement, well-understood, fast per step
  • Minimal dispersion required: IWB or Compact -- best accuracy per memory unit
  • Long-range propagation: High-Order O(4,2) -- lowest dispersion, allows coarser grids
  • Boundary modeling emphasis: DWM -- natural reflection coefficient treatment
  • Memory-constrained: Compact or IWB -- only 3N storage required

12. Applications

12.1 Room Acoustics

The simulator is applied to architectural acoustics problems including concert hall design, recording studio optimization, and classroom speech intelligibility analysis. Key capabilities:

  • Room impulse response (RIR) calculation by exciting with an impulse source
  • Reverberation time (RT60) estimation from the energy decay curve
  • Room mode visualization showing pressure distribution at resonant frequencies
  • Acoustic treatment optimization by comparing absorption coefficient configurations

For larger rooms, the compact and IWB schemes are recommended to minimize the number of grid points needed per wavelength, significantly reducing memory and computation time.

12.2 Underwater Acoustics

With appropriate medium properties (\(c_0 \approx 1500\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1000\) kg/m^3), the code simulates marine environments. Applications include:

  • Cetacean echolocation click propagation and beam pattern analysis
  • SONAR system modeling and performance prediction
  • Underwater noise impact assessment for marine life
  • Acoustic channel characterization for ocean communication

The higher speed of sound in water means that for the same frequency, wavelengths are approximately 4.4x longer than in air, favoring coarser grids and larger domains.

12.3 Bioacoustics

Preset scenarios model biological sound production mechanisms:

Cetacean Phonation

Dolphin and whale vocalizations are simulated using phased source arrays representing the phonic lips and melon structures [10, 11]. The melon acts as an acoustic lens, producing the characteristic beam patterns observed in odontocete echolocation clicks. The simulator can predict the far-field beam pattern from the near-field source geometry.

Avian Syrinx

Bird song production is modeled following the nonlinear dynamics approach of Mindlin and colleagues [12, 13]. The syrinx is represented as a pair of sound sources with coupled dynamics, producing the complex harmonic and subharmonic patterns observed in songbirds such as the zebra finch and canary.

13. Conclusion and Future Work

We have presented a comprehensive multi-algorithm framework for three-dimensional acoustic wave propagation simulation. The five implemented methods -- Standard FDTD, Compact KW, Interpolated Wideband, Digital Waveguide Mesh, and High-Order FDTD -- each offer distinct trade-offs between computational cost, memory requirements, accuracy, and implementation complexity.

Key findings from the benchmark comparison:

  1. The compact and IWB schemes provide the best accuracy-to-memory ratio, requiring only 3 floats per node while achieving significantly reduced dispersion.
  2. The high-order scheme delivers the lowest overall dispersion at the cost of a stricter CFL condition and wider stencil, making it ideal for long-range propagation.
  3. The DWM provides a physically intuitive formulation with guaranteed passivity, but has the highest memory cost (13 floats per node).
  4. The standard FDTD remains a strong general-purpose choice with the simplest implementation and competitive performance.
  5. Total simulation cost (FLOPs x steps) is comparable across schemes for equivalent accuracy targets, as higher per-step cost is offset by fewer steps or coarser grids.

Future work will extend the framework with:

  • Pseudo-Spectral Time-Domain (PSTD) and k-space methods using FFT-based spatial derivatives
  • Adaptive Rectangular Decomposition (ARD) for efficient large-domain simulation
  • Perfectly Matched Layer (PML) absorbing boundaries for improved open-boundary modeling
  • Frequency-dependent impedance boundary conditions using digital filter design
  • GPU acceleration via WebGPU compute shaders for real-time large-scale simulations
  • Adaptive mesh refinement for geometrically complex domains
  • Coupling with aeroacoustic models for flow-noise interaction

The open-source nature of this project invites community contributions. All source code, documentation, benchmarks, and example scenarios are available at the project repository.
