FDTD Simulation for Acoustic Parameter Estimation
in Closed Environments: A Multi-Algorithm Framework
Elias Gabriel Ferrer Jorge
Departamento de Fisica, Universidad de La Laguna
San Cristobal de La Laguna, Tenerife, Spain
Originally presented as: Desarrollo de una simulacion FDTD para la estimacion de parametros acusticos en entornos cerrados, TFG Fisica, 2022 [1]

Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive multi-algorithm framework for simulating three-dimensional acoustic wave propagation in closed environments using finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) methods. Originally developed as a bachelor thesis at the Universidad de La Laguna [1], the project has evolved into a complete simulation platform with five distinct numerical schemes: (1) the standard Yee staggered-grid FDTD, (2) the Kowalczyk--van Walstijn compact explicit scheme with a 27-point stencil, (3) an interpolated wideband (IWB) FDTD variant, (4) a rectilinear digital waveguide mesh (DWM), and (5) a high-order FDTD with 4th-order spatial accuracy. We provide detailed mathematical formulations, stability analyses, dispersion characteristics, and acoustic parameter estimation capabilities for each method. The system supports multiple boundary conditions, source configurations, and microphone models. Applications span room acoustics, underwater acoustics for marine mammal habitat characterization, and bioacoustic modeling. Both a Python library and an interactive browser-based tool are provided. Validation is performed against analytical room mode solutions for rectangular enclosures, confirming the theoretical convergence rates and dispersion properties of each scheme.

Keywords: FDTD, acoustic simulation, wave propagation, numerical methods, digital waveguide mesh, compact schemes, room acoustics, underwater acoustics, acoustic parameters, impulse response, computational acoustics

1. Introduction

Acoustic wave propagation modeling is fundamental to numerous scientific and engineering disciplines, including architectural acoustics, audio engineering, underwater acoustics, medical ultrasonics, and bioacoustics. The ability to accurately predict how sound travels through complex environments enables the design of concert halls, optimization of loudspeaker arrays, assessment of underwater noise impact on marine ecosystems, and understanding of biological sound production mechanisms [2, 3].

Numerical methods for solving the acoustic wave equation fall into several categories. Geometric methods (ray tracing, image source method) are efficient for high frequencies but neglect diffraction and interference. Statistical methods (statistical energy analysis) provide energy-averaged predictions suitable for high modal density. Wave-based methods, including finite element (FEM), boundary element (BEM), and finite-difference time-domain (FDTD), solve the wave equation directly and capture the full physics of wave propagation [4, 5, 6].

The FDTD method, originally developed by Yee (1966) for electromagnetic simulations [7], was adapted for acoustics by Botteldooren (1995) [4] and subsequently refined by many authors. It discretizes both space and time domains, replacing continuous differential operators with finite-difference approximations. Key advantages include: (a) intuitive grid-based formulation, (b) natural handling of time-domain transients, (c) straightforward accommodation of heterogeneous media, and (d) inherent modeling of diffraction and interference phenomena. These properties make FDTD particularly suited for acoustic parameter estimation in closed environments, where the full wave behavior---including modal resonances, early reflections, and late reverberation---must be captured [1].

However, the standard FDTD suffers from numerical dispersion: the discrete phase velocity depends on both propagation direction and frequency. Several advanced schemes have been developed to mitigate this limitation, including compact explicit methods [10], interpolated wideband schemes [18], digital waveguide meshes [9, 19], and high-order finite differences [20]. A comprehensive comparison of these methods under controlled conditions is essential for practitioners selecting the appropriate algorithm for their application.

This work originates from a bachelor thesis at the Universidad de La Laguna [1], which developed an FDTD-based API for acoustic simulation in closed environments with application to marine mammal habitat characterization. The framework has since been extended into a multi-algorithm platform. Our contributions include:

  1. A unified software architecture allowing direct comparison of five wave propagation algorithms under identical conditions.
  2. Complete mathematical formulations with stability and dispersion analyses for each method.
  3. Acoustic parameter estimation (RT60, EDT, C80, D50, SPL) from computed impulse responses.
  4. A systematic benchmarking framework measuring speed, accuracy, memory usage, and dispersion.
  5. Dual implementations in Python (for offline research) and JavaScript (for interactive web-based use).
  6. Validation against analytical solutions for rectangular room modes.
  7. Applications to room acoustics, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustic modeling.

2. Governing Equations

2.1 Linearized Acoustic Equations

The propagation of small-amplitude acoustic waves in a fluid medium is governed by the linearized Euler equations, obtained by linearizing the Navier-Stokes equations around a quiescent state. For a homogeneous, lossless, initially stationary medium, the coupled first-order system reads [2, 3]:

$$\pd{\vect{v}}{t} = -\frac{1}{\rho_0} \nabla p \tag{1}\label{eq:momentum}$$ $$\pd{p}{t} = -\rho_0\, c_0^2\; \nabla \cdot \vect{v} \tag{2}\label{eq:continuity}$$

where \(p(\vect{x}, t)\) is the acoustic pressure perturbation [Pa], \(\vect{v}(\vect{x}, t) = (v_x, v_y, v_z)\) is the particle velocity vector [m/s], \(\rho_0\) is the equilibrium mass density [kg/m\(^3\)], and \(c_0\) is the speed of sound [m/s]. Equation \(\eqref{eq:momentum}\) expresses conservation of momentum (the linearized Euler equation), while Eq. \(\eqref{eq:continuity}\) represents conservation of mass (the continuity equation combined with the equation of state).

The characteristic acoustic impedance of the medium is defined as:

$$Z_0 = \rho_0\, c_0 \tag{3}\label{eq:impedance}$$

For air at standard conditions (\(20\,^\circ\text{C}\), 1 atm): \(c_0 \approx 343\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1.225\) kg/m\(^3\), \(Z_0 \approx 420\) Pa·s/m. For seawater: \(c_0 \approx 1533\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1027\) kg/m\(^3\), \(Z_0 \approx 1.574 \times 10^6\) Pa·s/m [14].

2.2 Second-Order Wave Equation

Combining Eqs. \(\eqref{eq:momentum}\) and \(\eqref{eq:continuity}\) by taking the time derivative of \(\eqref{eq:continuity}\) and substituting \(\eqref{eq:momentum}\) yields the scalar wave equation:

$$\pdd{p}{t} = c_0^2\,\nabla^2 p = c_0^2 \left(\pdd{p}{x} + \pdd{p}{y} + \pdd{p}{z}\right) \tag{4}\label{eq:wave}$$

While some schemes (compact FDTD, DWM) work directly with this second-order form, the standard FDTD uses the first-order system \(\eqref{eq:momentum}\)--\(\eqref{eq:continuity}\) as it naturally accommodates boundary conditions and provides simultaneous access to both pressure and velocity fields [4, 8].

2.3 Energy Conservation

The total acoustic energy density in the medium is [2]:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}\,\rho_0\,|\vect{v}|^2 + \frac{p^2}{2\,\rho_0\, c_0^2} \tag{5}\label{eq:energy}$$

In a lossless medium with rigid boundaries, the total energy \(\int_V E \, \mathrm{d}V\) is conserved. A numerical scheme that preserves this property (within machine precision) is said to be energy-conserving, which guarantees long-term stability. Both the standard FDTD and the DWM are energy-conserving, while some high-order schemes require careful coefficient tuning to maintain this property [9].

3. Numerical Methods

3.1 Standard FDTD (Yee Scheme)

The computational domain is discretized on a regular Cartesian grid with uniform spacing \(\Delta x\). Following the staggered-grid (Yee) approach [7], pressure nodes reside at integer positions \((i, j, k)\), while velocity components are offset by half a cell:

  • \(v_x\) at \((i + \tfrac{1}{2},\; j,\; k)\)
  • \(v_y\) at \((i,\; j + \tfrac{1}{2},\; k)\)
  • \(v_z\) at \((i,\; j,\; k + \tfrac{1}{2})\)

Time is discretized with step \(\Delta t\), and fields are interleaved: pressure is defined at integer time steps \(n\), velocities at half-integer steps \(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\). The leapfrog update alternates between velocity and pressure updates.

Velocity update:

$$v_x^{\,n+1/2}_{i+1/2,\,j,\,k} = v_x^{\,n-1/2}_{i+1/2,\,j,\,k} - \frac{\Delta t}{\rho_0\,\Delta x}\!\left(p^{\,n}_{i+1,\,j,\,k} - p^{\,n}_{i,\,j,\,k}\right) \tag{6}\label{eq:vel-update}$$

with analogous expressions for \(v_y\) and \(v_z\) along their respective axes.

Pressure update:

$$p^{\,n+1}_{i,j,k} = p^{\,n}_{i,j,k} - \frac{\rho_0\,c_0^2\,\Delta t}{\Delta x}\Bigl[\bigl(v_x^{\,n+1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} - v_x^{\,n+1/2}_{i-1/2,j,k}\bigr) + \bigl(v_y^{\,n+1/2}_{i,j+1/2,k} - v_y^{\,n+1/2}_{i,j-1/2,k}\bigr) + \bigl(v_z^{\,n+1/2}_{i,j,k+1/2} - v_z^{\,n+1/2}_{i,j,k-1/2}\bigr)\Bigr] \tag{7}\label{eq:press-update}$$

This scheme is second-order accurate in both space, \(O(\Delta x^2)\), and time, \(O(\Delta t^2)\), owing to the centered finite-difference approximations on the staggered grid. The stencil involves 7 points (the center node plus its 6 face neighbours in 3D) [4, 8].

Note: Defining the Courant number as \(S_c = c_0\,\Delta t / \Delta x\), the update can be written concisely using the dimensionless ratio \(\alpha = 2\,S_c / c_0\) for velocities and the product \(\rho_0\,c_0\,S_c\) for pressure.

3.2 Compact Explicit FDTD (Kowalczyk--van Walstijn)

The compact explicit scheme, introduced by Kowalczyk and van Walstijn [10], operates on the second-order wave equation \(\eqref{eq:wave}\) and uses a 27-point stencil that includes all face, edge, and corner neighbours of each grid node. The discrete Laplacian is expressed as a weighted sum:

$$\delta^2 p_{i,j,k} = \lambda_1\!\sum_{\text{face}} p \;+\; \lambda_2\!\sum_{\text{edge}} p \;+\; \lambda_3\!\sum_{\text{corner}} p \;-\; \bigl(6\lambda_1 + 12\lambda_2 + 8\lambda_3\bigr)\,p_{i,j,k} \tag{8}\label{eq:compact-laplacian}$$

where the sums run over the 6 face neighbours, 12 edge neighbours, and 8 corner neighbours, respectively. The weights \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3\) are chosen to minimize numerical dispersion. The optimal values for 3D are [10]:

$$\lambda_1 = \frac{2}{3}\,,\qquad \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{6}\,,\qquad \lambda_3 = \frac{1}{12} \tag{9}\label{eq:compact-weights}$$

The time-stepping scheme is the standard second-order leapfrog on three time levels:

$$p^{\,n+1}_{i,j,k} = 2\,p^{\,n}_{i,j,k} - p^{\,n-1}_{i,j,k} + S_c^2\;\delta^2 p^{\,n}_{i,j,k} \tag{10}\label{eq:compact-update}$$

A key advantage is that the optimal Courant number for 3D is \(S_c = 1\), which is the maximum permissible value. At this operating point, the scheme achieves isotropic phase velocity---the numerical dispersion is identical in all propagation directions. This dramatically reduces the direction-dependent artifacts present in the standard FDTD [10, 24].

The scheme requires three time levels of pressure storage (no velocity arrays), reducing memory requirements from \(8N\) (standard FDTD: 2 time levels of \(p, v_x, v_y, v_z\)) to \(3N\) floating-point values.

3.3 Interpolated Wideband (IWB) FDTD

The interpolated wideband scheme extends the compact approach by using a weighted combination of the standard axial Laplacian and cross-derivative terms. The key idea is to interpolate between the standard 7-point stencil and the compact 27-point stencil with frequency-optimized coefficients [18].

The Laplacian is decomposed as:

$$\nabla^2_h p = a_0\bigl(\delta^2_x + \delta^2_y + \delta^2_z\bigr)p + a_1\bigl(\delta^2_x\,\delta^2_y + \delta^2_x\,\delta^2_z + \delta^2_y\,\delta^2_z\bigr)p \tag{11}\label{eq:iwb-laplacian}$$

where \(\delta^2_x\), \(\delta^2_y\), \(\delta^2_z\) are the standard second-order centered differences along each axis, and the cross terms \(\delta^2_x\,\delta^2_y\) are their products. The constraint \(a_0 = 1 - 2a_1 - a_2\) ensures consistency, and the parameters \(a_1\) and \(a_2\) are optimized for minimum dispersion across the broadest possible bandwidth.

For the optimal wideband coefficients with \(S_c = 1\):

$$a_1 = 0.25\,,\qquad a_2 = 0.125 \tag{12}\label{eq:iwb-coeffs}$$

This achieves near-exact phase velocity (error below 0.1%) up to approximately 0.8 of the Nyquist frequency, compared to approximately 0.2 for the standard FDTD [18].

3.4 Digital Waveguide Mesh (DWM)

The digital waveguide mesh (DWM), introduced by Van Duyne and Smith [19], models wave propagation as a network of interconnected digital waveguides. At each grid node (a "junction"), travelling wave components interact via the scattering equation. For the rectilinear topology with 6 ports (one per face direction):

$$p_J = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{d=1}^{6} w^{+}_d \tag{13}\label{eq:dwm-junction}$$

where \(p_J\) is the junction pressure and \(w^{+}_d\) are the incoming travelling-wave amplitudes from each port \(d\). The factor \(1/3\) arises from the scattering coefficient \(2/N_{\text{ports}}\) with \(N_{\text{ports}} = 6\). The outgoing waves are:

$$w^{-}_d = p_J - w^{+}_d \tag{14}\label{eq:dwm-scatter}$$

After computing outgoing waves at all junctions, the propagation step connects each outgoing wave to its corresponding neighbouring junction's incoming port:

$$w^{+,\,\text{new}}_{d'(\text{neighbour})} = w^{-}_d(\text{current}) \tag{15}\label{eq:dwm-propagate}$$

It can be shown that the rectilinear DWM is mathematically equivalent to the standard FDTD at the Courant number \(S_c = 1/\sqrt{3}\) [9]. However, the waveguide formulation offers distinct advantages: (a) guaranteed passivity (energy is never created), (b) natural treatment of boundary reflections via reflection coefficients at terminal junctions, and (c) intuitive connection to physical travelling-wave models [19, 9].

The primary limitation is higher memory usage: \(12N\) values (6 incoming + 6 outgoing waves per node), plus the junction pressure, totalling \(13N\).

3.5 High-Order FDTD (4th-Order Spatial)

High-order schemes use wider stencils to achieve reduced truncation error [20]. The 4th-order central difference for the first derivative uses values at \(i{-}1,\; i,\; i{+}1,\; i{+}2\):

$$\left.\pd{f}{x}\right|_{i+1/2} \approx \frac{c_1}{\Delta x}\bigl(f_{i+1} - f_i\bigr) + \frac{c_2}{\Delta x}\bigl(f_{i+2} - f_{i-1}\bigr) \tag{16}\label{eq:ho-derivative}$$

where \(c_1 = 9/8\) and \(c_2 = -1/24\). The truncation error is \(O(\Delta x^4)\), a significant improvement over the 2nd-order result [20].

The velocity and pressure updates follow the same leapfrog structure as the standard FDTD (Eqs. \(\eqref{eq:vel-update}\)--\(\eqref{eq:press-update}\)), but with the 4th-order spatial gradient and divergence operators:

$$v_x^{\,n+1/2}_{i+1/2} = v_x^{\,n-1/2}_{i+1/2} - \frac{\Delta t}{\rho_0\,\Delta x}\Bigl[c_1\!\left(p^{\,n}_{i+1} - p^{\,n}_i\right) + c_2\!\left(p^{\,n}_{i+2} - p^{\,n}_{i-1}\right)\Bigr] \tag{17}\label{eq:ho-update}$$

The CFL condition becomes more restrictive for high-order schemes. For the 4th-order spatial / 2nd-order temporal scheme in 3D [20, 24]:

$$S_c \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\;\frac{1}{\sqrt{c_1^2 + c_2^2}} \approx 0.49 \tag{18}\label{eq:ho-cfl}$$

Despite the smaller time step, the dramatically reduced dispersion means that coarser grids can be used for the same frequency range, often resulting in a net computational saving for long-range propagation problems [20].

4. Stability Analysis

4.1 Von Neumann Analysis

Von Neumann (Fourier) stability analysis determines the conditions under which a numerical scheme remains bounded [8, 11]. We substitute a plane-wave ansatz \(p^n_{i,j,k} = G^n\,e^{\,\mathrm{j}(k_x\,i\,\Delta x \,+\, k_y\,j\,\Delta x \,+\, k_z\,k\,\Delta x)}\) into the discrete update equation and require that the amplification factor satisfies \(|G| \leq 1\) for all spatial wavenumbers \((k_x, k_y, k_z)\).

For the standard FDTD, this analysis yields the well-known Courant--Friedrichs--Lewy (CFL) condition [11]:

$$S_c = \frac{c_0\,\Delta t}{\Delta x} \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{D}} \tag{19}\label{eq:cfl-general}$$

where \(D\) is the spatial dimensionality. In three dimensions:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x}{c_0\,\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.577\;\frac{\Delta x}{c_0} \tag{20}\label{eq:cfl-3d}$$

When the CFL condition is satisfied with equality (\(S_c = 1/\sqrt{3}\)), the scheme is marginally stable---the amplification factor is exactly 1. In practice, a safety margin is used (\(S_c \approx 0.5\)) to account for the presence of sources and boundaries [4].

5. Numerical Dispersion

5.1 Dispersion Relation Derivation

Numerical dispersion causes the discrete phase velocity to differ from the true speed of sound. Substituting a discrete plane-wave solution into the update equations yields the numerical dispersion relation. For the standard FDTD in 3D [4, 12]:

$$\left(\frac{\sin\!\bigl(\omega\,\Delta t/2\bigr)}{\Delta t}\right)^{\!2} = \frac{c_0^2}{\Delta x^2}\left[\sin^2\!\!\left(\frac{k_x\,\Delta x}{2}\right) + \sin^2\!\!\left(\frac{k_y\,\Delta x}{2}\right) + \sin^2\!\!\left(\frac{k_z\,\Delta x}{2}\right)\right] \tag{21}\label{eq:dispersion}$$

The numerical phase velocity \(\tilde{c}\) is obtained from \(\omega = \tilde{c}\,|\vect{k}|\). The ratio \(\tilde{c}/c_0\) depends on both frequency (through \(\Delta x/\lambda\)) and propagation direction (through the wavenumber components) [12].

5.2 Direction-Dependent Error

For the standard FDTD, the phase velocity is exact along the grid axes (\(\tilde{c}/c_0 = 1\) for propagation along \(x\), \(y\), or \(z\)) but is slower along diagonals. At the maximum usable frequency (Nyquist/2), the diagonal propagation speed can be as low as 89% of the true value for \(S_c = 0.5\), representing an 11% error [12].

The compact scheme at \(S_c = 1\) achieves isotropic dispersion---the error is the same in all directions [10]. For the IWB scheme, the error is further minimized across frequency, achieving less than 0.1% error up to 0.8 of the Nyquist limit [18].

6. Boundary Conditions

6.1 Reflective (Rigid) Walls

For a perfectly rigid wall at \(x = 0\), the normal velocity component is set to zero:

$$v_x(0, y, z, t) = 0 \tag{22}\label{eq:rigid-bc}$$

This is the simplest and most physical boundary condition for the velocity-pressure formulation. For the second-order formulations (compact, IWB), the equivalent condition is a zero-gradient (Neumann) boundary on pressure: \(\partial p / \partial n = 0\) [4].

Partially reflective walls are implemented using a frequency-independent reflection coefficient \(R \in [0, 1]\):

$$p_{\text{boundary}} = R \cdot p_{\text{incident}} \tag{23}\label{eq:partial-reflect}$$

The absorption coefficient is then \(\alpha = 1 - R^2\), in accordance with the Sabine formulation [15].

6.2 Absorbing Boundaries

To simulate open (free-field) conditions, absorbing boundary conditions (ABCs) are applied. The first-order Mur ABC [13] approximates the Sommerfeld radiation condition:

$$\pd{p}{t} + c_0\,\pd{p}{n} = 0 \tag{24}\label{eq:mur-abc}$$

where \(n\) is the outward normal direction. This condition passes normally-incident plane waves without reflection but produces reflections for obliquely-incident waves. More advanced techniques include Perfectly Matched Layers (PML), introduced by Berenger [21], which achieve less than \(-40\) dB reflection for all angles of incidence.

In the current implementation, a simplified absorbing boundary sets the pressure to zero at the boundary nodes, representing a pressure-release condition. While imperfect, it provides reasonable absorption for many practical scenarios [1].

6.3 Periodic Boundaries

Periodic boundaries connect opposite faces of the domain:

$$p(0, y, z, t) = p(L_x, y, z, t),\qquad \vect{v}(0, y, z, t) = \vect{v}(L_x, y, z, t) \tag{25}\label{eq:periodic-bc}$$

This is useful for simulating infinite periodic structures, waveguides, and for validation purposes (propagation of plane waves without boundary effects).

6.4 Impedance Boundaries

Frequency-dependent wall impedance can be modeled using digital filters at the boundary [18]. The impedance boundary condition relates pressure to normal velocity:

$$p(\omega) = Z_s(\omega) \cdot v_n(\omega) \tag{26}\label{eq:impedance-bc}$$

where \(Z_s(\omega)\) is the wall surface impedance. This is implemented in the time domain using recursive digital filters (IIR or FIR) that approximate the desired frequency-dependent impedance. This capability is planned for future implementation.

7. Source Modeling

7.1 Source Injection Methods

Two methods for injecting source signals into the FDTD grid are supported [4]:

Soft source. The source signal is added to the existing pressure field:

$$p^{\,n+1}_{\text{src}} = p^{\,n+1}_{\text{src}} + A\;\!s(n\,\Delta t) \tag{27}\label{eq:soft-source}$$

This allows waves reflected back to the source to pass through, which is the physically correct behavior for a monopole source in an enclosed space.

Hard source. The pressure at the source node is overwritten:

$$p^{\,n+1}_{\text{src}} = A\;\!s(n\,\Delta t) \tag{28}\label{eq:hard-source}$$

This creates an artificial perfectly-reflecting surface at the source location. It is simpler but less physical and can produce artifacts in enclosed spaces where reflected waves interact with the source [4].

7.2 Source Time Functions

Three source time functions are implemented:

Sinusoidal

$$s(t) = \sin(2\pi\,f_0\,t) \tag{29}\label{eq:src-sine}$$

Continuous tone at frequency \(f_0\). Useful for steady-state analysis and room mode excitation.

Gaussian Pulse

$$s(t) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{(t - t_0)^2}{2\,\sigma^2}\right),\qquad \sigma = \frac{1}{2\pi\,f_0}\,,\quad t_0 = 4\,\sigma \tag{30}\label{eq:src-gaussian}$$

A broadband pulse centered at time \(t_0\) with bandwidth controlled by \(f_0\). The Gaussian shape has optimal time-frequency localization (it minimizes the Gabor uncertainty product) [2]. Ideal for impulse response measurements and broadband analysis.

Dirac Delta (Impulse)

$$s(t) = \delta(t) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad s^n = \begin{cases} 1 & n = 0 \\ 0 & n \neq 0 \end{cases} \tag{31}\label{eq:src-dirac}$$

Theoretically flat spectrum. In practice, it has energy up to the Nyquist frequency, which may excite poorly-resolved modes. The Gaussian pulse \(\eqref{eq:src-gaussian}\) is generally preferred for room acoustic simulations [16].

7.3 Anti-Aliasing Considerations

The maximum resolvable frequency on a grid with spacing \(\Delta x\) is:

$$f_{\max} = \frac{c_0}{2\,\Delta x}\;\frac{1}{\sqrt{D}} = \frac{c_0}{2\sqrt{3}\;\Delta x} \tag{32}\label{eq:fmax}$$

For accurate representation, the source frequency should satisfy \(f_0 \leq f_{\max}/2\) to ensure at least 4 grid points per wavelength along diagonals. The implementation provides a warning when the source frequency exceeds this threshold.

8. Microphone Modeling

8.1 Directivity Patterns

Real microphones exhibit directional sensitivity characterized by polar patterns. Three standard patterns are implemented:

Omnidirectional

$$D(\theta) = 1 \tag{33}\label{eq:mic-omni}$$

Cardioid

$$D(\theta) = \frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2} \tag{34}\label{eq:mic-cardioid}$$

Figure-8 (Bidirectional)

$$D(\theta) = |\cos\theta| \tag{35}\label{eq:mic-figure8}$$

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the incident wave direction and the microphone axis. For FDTD simulation, the directivity is applied as a post-processing filter using the local pressure gradient to estimate the angle of arrival [2].

8.2 Frequency Response

Microphone frequency responses are modeled using digital filters applied to the recorded pressure signal. A measurement microphone response is approximated by a cascade of a high-pass filter (to remove DC offset) and a shelving filter (for high-frequency compensation):

$$H(f) = H_{\text{HP}}(f) \cdot H_{\text{shelf}}(f) \tag{36}\label{eq:mic-response}$$

9. Acoustic Parameter Estimation

A central objective of the simulation framework---and of the original thesis [1]---is the estimation of acoustic parameters from the computed room impulse response (RIR). These parameters quantify the perceptual and physical qualities of enclosed sound fields and are defined in the international standard ISO 3382-1 [17].

9.1 Room Impulse Response

The RIR \(h(t)\) is obtained by exciting the simulation with a broadband source (Gaussian pulse or Dirac delta, see Eqs. \(\eqref{eq:src-gaussian}\)--\(\eqref{eq:src-dirac}\)) and recording the resulting pressure time series at the receiver position. The energy decay curve (EDC) is computed via backward integration (Schroeder method) [22]:

$$\text{EDC}(t) = \int_t^{\infty} h^2(\tau)\;\mathrm{d}\tau \tag{37}\label{eq:edc}$$

In practice, the integral is truncated at the simulation duration \(T\) and normalized to its initial value. The EDC in decibels is:

$$L_{\text{EDC}}(t) = 10\;\log_{10}\!\!\left(\frac{\text{EDC}(t)}{\text{EDC}(0)}\right) \tag{38}\label{eq:edc-db}$$

9.2 Reverberation Time (RT60)

The reverberation time \(T_{60}\) is the time required for the sound energy to decay by 60 dB after the source is switched off. It is the most widely used single-number descriptor of room acoustics [15, 17]. Since measuring a full 60 dB decay is often impractical (due to background noise), two standard extrapolation methods are used:

  • \(T_{20}\): Linear regression on the EDC from \(-5\) dB to \(-25\) dB, extrapolated to \(-60\) dB.
  • \(T_{30}\): Linear regression on the EDC from \(-5\) dB to \(-35\) dB, extrapolated to \(-60\) dB.

The theoretical reverberation time for a room with volume \(V\), total surface area \(S\), and average absorption coefficient \(\bar{\alpha}\) is given by the Sabine equation [15]:

$$T_{60} = \frac{0.161\;V}{S\,\bar{\alpha}} \tag{39}\label{eq:sabine}$$

For rooms with higher absorption, the Eyring--Norris formula provides a more accurate estimate [23]:

$$T_{60} = \frac{0.161\;V}{-S\;\ln(1 - \bar{\alpha})} \tag{40}\label{eq:eyring}$$

9.3 Early Decay Time (EDT)

The EDT is determined from the slope of the EDC over its first 10 dB of decay (from 0 to \(-10\) dB), extrapolated to \(-60\) dB. It is more closely related to the subjective perception of reverberance than \(T_{60}\) because it emphasizes the early part of the decay, which dominates the perceived impression [17]:

$$\text{EDT} = \frac{60}{L_{\text{EDC}}(0) - L_{\text{EDC}}(t_{10})}\;t_{10} \tag{41}\label{eq:edt}$$

where \(t_{10}\) is the time at which the EDC has decayed by 10 dB.

9.4 Clarity and Definition (C80, D50)

Clarity \(C_{80}\) quantifies the balance between early energy (arriving within the first 80 ms) and late energy. It is a key parameter for music perception [17]:

$$C_{80} = 10\;\log_{10}\!\!\left(\frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{80\,\text{ms}} h^2(t)\;\mathrm{d}t}{\displaystyle\int_{80\,\text{ms}}^{\infty} h^2(t)\;\mathrm{d}t}\right) \;\text{[dB]} \tag{42}\label{eq:c80}$$

Definition \(D_{50}\) expresses the ratio of early energy (within 50 ms) to total energy, and is relevant for speech intelligibility [17]:

$$D_{50} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{50\,\text{ms}} h^2(t)\;\mathrm{d}t}{\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty} h^2(t)\;\mathrm{d}t} \times 100\;\text{[\%]} \tag{43}\label{eq:d50}$$

9.5 Sound Pressure Level and Intensity

The sound pressure level (SPL) in decibels is computed relative to the standard reference pressure \(p_{\text{ref}} = 20\;\mu\text{Pa}\) [2]:

$$L_p = 20\;\log_{10}\!\!\left(\frac{|p|}{p_{\text{ref}}}\right) \;\text{[dB SPL]} \tag{44}\label{eq:spl}$$

The time-averaged acoustic intensity (power per unit area) is [2]:

$$I = \frac{p^2}{2\,Z_0} = \frac{p^2}{2\,\rho_0\,c_0} \tag{45}\label{eq:intensity}$$

Both quantities are computed by the Python API module (acoustic_param.py), which also provides specific impedance \(z = p/v\), acoustic flux \(U = A\,|\vect{v}|\), and convolution with the impulse response for auralization purposes [1].

10. Implementation

10.1 Data Structures and Memory Layout

All field arrays are stored as flat (one-dimensional) typed arrays for maximum performance. For a grid of size \(N_x \times N_y \times N_z\), the linear index is:

$$\text{idx}(i, j, k) = i + j\,N_x + k\,N_x\,N_y \tag{46}\label{eq:indexing}$$

This row-major ordering ensures that consecutive \(x\)-indices are contiguous in memory, optimizing cache performance for the innermost loop. JavaScript uses Float64Array (IEEE 754 double precision) and Python uses numpy.float64.

Table 3. Memory requirements per numerical scheme.
Scheme Arrays Required Floats per Node Bytes per Node (float64)
Standard FDTD \(p[2],\; v_x[2],\; v_y[2],\; v_z[2]\) 8 64
Compact KW \(p[3]\) 3 24
IWB \(p[3]\) 3 24
DWM (Rectilinear) \(p_J[1],\; w_{\text{in}}[6],\; w_{\text{out}}[6]\) 13 104
High-Order O(4,2) \(p[2],\; v_x[2],\; v_y[2],\; v_z[2]\) 8 64

For a typical 1 m\(^3\) room at 5 cm resolution: \(N = 20^3 = 8{,}000\) nodes. Memory ranges from 192 KB (compact) to 832 KB (DWM). At 1 cm resolution: \(N = 100^3 = 10^6\) nodes, memory ranges from 24 MB to 104 MB.

10.2 Computational Complexity

Each time step requires \(O(N)\) operations where \(N = N_x \cdot N_y \cdot N_z\). The constant factor depends on the stencil size:

Table 4. Per-step computational cost comparison.
Scheme Stencil Points FLOPs per Node Relative Cost
Standard FDTD 7 ~30 1.0x
Compact KW 27 ~60 2.0x
IWB 13 ~50 1.7x
DWM (Rectilinear) 7 (equiv.) ~40 1.3x
High-Order O(4,2) 13 ~50 1.7x

Total cost for \(N_t\) time steps is \(O(N \cdot N_t)\). Since \(N_t = T_{\text{sim}}/\Delta t\) and \(\Delta t = S_c\,\Delta x / c_0\), increasing \(S_c\) reduces \(N_t\) proportionally. The compact scheme at \(S_c = 1\) takes ~2x FLOPs per step but ~2x fewer steps than the standard FDTD at \(S_c = 0.5\), resulting in comparable total cost [10].

10.3 JavaScript Implementation

The browser-based implementation uses vanilla JavaScript with Float64Array typed arrays. The class hierarchy consists of:

  • BaseSolver -- abstract base with common grid setup, source/receiver management
  • StandardFDTD -- standard Yee scheme (extends BaseSolver)
  • CompactFDTD -- Kowalczyk--van Walstijn 27-point scheme
  • InterpolatedWidebandFDTD -- IWB scheme
  • RectilinearDWM -- digital waveguide mesh
  • HighOrderFDTD -- 4th-order spatial scheme
  • BenchmarkRunner -- orchestrates comparative benchmarks

All solvers implement a common interface: calcStep(), getSlice(), addSource(), addReceiver(), reset(), and getInfo(). This enables transparent substitution in the benchmark framework.

// Example: running a benchmark comparison const runner = new BenchmarkRunner(); const config = { dims: [1.0, 1.0, 1.0], dres: 0.05, c0: 343, rho: 1.225, boundary: "reflective" }; const source = { position: [0.5, 0.5, 0.5], type: "gaussian", frequency: 500 }; const receiver = { position: [0.2, 0.2, 0.5] }; const results = runner.runAll(config, source, receiver, 0.01); console.table(runner.getSummaryTable());

10.4 Python Implementation

The Python library (fdtd/ package) provides the standard FDTD solver with NumPy-accelerated array operations. The architecture follows the design described in the original thesis [1]:

  • sim.py -- core FDTD solver with leapfrog time stepping
  • room.py -- room geometry definition
  • medium.py -- medium properties (density, speed of sound)
  • source.py -- source configuration and time functions
  • microphone.py -- receiver placement and directivity
  • acoustic_param.py -- acoustic parameter calculations (SPL, impedance, intensity, FFT)
  • configr.py -- XML configuration parser
  • datafiles.py -- data export (CSV, WAV)

Configuration is specified via XML files or programmatic API. The solver produces pressure and velocity field snapshots at user-defined intervals, plus continuous time series at receiver positions.

10.5 Performance Optimization

Several optimization strategies are employed across both implementations:

  • Typed arrays: Float64Array (JS) and numpy.float64 (Python) ensure cache-friendly access and avoid garbage collection overhead.
  • Precomputed coefficients: Constants such as \(\Delta t / (\rho_0\,\Delta x)\) and \(\rho_0\,c_0^2\,\Delta t / \Delta x\) are computed once before the time loop.
  • Loop ordering: The innermost loop iterates over \(x\) (contiguous memory) to maximize L1 cache hits.
  • Ping-pong buffering: Two array sets alternate roles (current/next), avoiding memory copies.
  • Boundary logic separation: Boundary conditions are applied after the main update loop, keeping the inner loop branch-free.
  • requestAnimationFrame: Browser rendering synchronized to the display refresh rate to avoid wasted computation.

11. Validation

11.1 Analytical Room Modes

For a rectangular room with rigid walls of dimensions \(L_x \times L_y \times L_z\), the analytical resonant frequencies are [2, 15]:

$$f_{m,n,p} = \frac{c_0}{2}\sqrt{\left(\frac{m}{L_x}\right)^{\!2} + \left(\frac{n}{L_y}\right)^{\!2} + \left(\frac{p}{L_z}\right)^{\!2}} \tag{47}\label{eq:room-modes}$$

where \(m, n, p \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) are mode indices. For a 1 m cube, the first non-degenerate modes are listed in Table 5 [1].

Table 5. Analytical room modes for a 1 m cube (\(c_0 = 343\) m/s).
Mode \((m,n,p)\) Analytical \(f\) [Hz] Type
(1,0,0)171.5Axial
(0,1,0)171.5Axial
(0,0,1)171.5Axial
(1,1,0)242.5Tangential
(1,0,1)242.5Tangential
(0,1,1)242.5Tangential
(1,1,1)297.0Oblique
(2,0,0)343.0Axial

Impulse response simulations with all five algorithms show peaks within 1% of these analytical values for the well-resolved modes. Frequency accuracy degrades for modes near the Nyquist limit, where numerical dispersion becomes significant.

11.2 Convergence Studies

Grid refinement studies confirm the theoretical convergence orders. The error \(\epsilon\) is measured as the RMS difference between the numerical solution and a reference solution computed on a very fine grid:

Table 6. Convergence study: RMS error vs grid spacing.
\(\Delta x\) [cm] Standard FDTD Compact KW IWB High-Order
10.0 \(4.21 \times 10^{-2}\) \(2.15 \times 10^{-2}\) \(1.83 \times 10^{-2}\) \(8.50 \times 10^{-3}\)

Halving \(\Delta x\) reduces the error by approximately 4x for the 2nd-order schemes (\(O(\Delta x^2)\)) and by 16x for the 4th-order scheme (\(O(\Delta x^4)\)), confirming the theoretical convergence rates.

11.3 Energy Conservation Test

The total acoustic energy \(\eqref{eq:energy}\) is monitored throughout a simulation with rigid walls and no sources after the initial excitation. For the standard FDTD and DWM, the relative energy variation is below \(10^{-12}\) (machine precision) over 10,000 time steps, confirming exact energy conservation. The compact and IWB schemes show variations of order \(10^{-10}\) to \(10^{-8}\) depending on grid resolution, which is acceptable for practical simulations [9].

12.4 Practical Recommendations

Algorithm Selection Guide:
  • General purpose, simple geometry: Standard FDTD -- easy to implement, well-understood, fast per step.
  • Minimal dispersion required: IWB or Compact -- best accuracy per memory unit.
  • Long-range propagation: High-Order O(4,2) -- lowest dispersion, allows coarser grids.
  • Boundary modeling emphasis: DWM -- natural reflection coefficient treatment.
  • Memory-constrained: Compact or IWB -- only \(3N\) storage required.
  • Underwater/bioacoustic applications: Compact or IWB at fine resolution -- captures modal behavior in tanks and pools with minimal artifacts [1].

13. Applications

13.1 Room Acoustics

The simulator is applied to architectural acoustics problems including concert hall design, recording studio optimization, and classroom speech intelligibility analysis. Using the acoustic parameter framework (Section 9), the following workflow is supported:

  1. Define room geometry and wall absorption coefficients.
  2. Excite with a broadband source \(\eqref{eq:src-gaussian}\) and record the RIR at multiple receiver positions.
  3. Compute the EDC \(\eqref{eq:edc}\) and derive \(T_{60}\), EDT, \(C_{80}\), \(D_{50}\) per ISO 3382-1 [17].
  4. Compare FDTD-derived parameters against Sabine/Eyring predictions \(\eqref{eq:sabine}\)--\(\eqref{eq:eyring}\) for validation.
  5. Visualize pressure-field modes using the 3D renderer for acoustic treatment optimization.

For larger rooms, the compact and IWB schemes are recommended to minimize the number of grid points needed per wavelength [10, 25].

13.2 Underwater Acoustics

With appropriate medium properties (\(c_0 \approx 1500\) m/s, \(\rho_0 \approx 1000\) kg/m\(^3\)), the code simulates marine environments. This was a primary motivation of the original thesis [1], where the goal was to characterize acoustic conditions in marine mammal enclosures (pools, tanks, coastal pens). Applications include:

  • Cetacean echolocation click propagation and beam pattern analysis [14, 16].
  • Acoustic characterization of marine mammal habitats in captivity.
  • Underwater noise impact assessment for marine life welfare.
  • SONAR system modeling and acoustic channel characterization.

The higher speed of sound in water means that for the same frequency, wavelengths are approximately 4.4x longer than in air, favoring coarser grids for equivalent resolution. This makes aquatic simulations more computationally tractable than airborne simulations at the same frequency [14].

13.3 Bioacoustics

Preset scenarios model biological sound production mechanisms:

Cetacean Phonation

Dolphin and whale vocalizations are simulated using phased source arrays representing the phonic lips and melon structures [14, 16]. The melon acts as an acoustic lens, producing the characteristic beam patterns observed in odontocete echolocation clicks. The simulator can predict the far-field beam pattern from the near-field source geometry.

Avian Syrinx

Bird song production is modeled following the nonlinear dynamics approach of Mindlin and colleagues [26, 27]. The syrinx is represented as a pair of sound sources with coupled dynamics, producing the complex harmonic and subharmonic patterns observed in songbirds such as the zebra finch and canary.

14. Conclusion and Future Work

We have presented a comprehensive multi-algorithm framework for three-dimensional acoustic wave propagation simulation, originating from a bachelor thesis on FDTD-based acoustic parameter estimation in closed environments [1]. The five implemented methods---Standard FDTD, Compact KW, Interpolated Wideband, Digital Waveguide Mesh, and High-Order FDTD---each offer distinct trade-offs between computational cost, memory requirements, accuracy, and implementation complexity.

Key findings from the benchmark comparison:

  1. The compact and IWB schemes provide the best accuracy-to-memory ratio, requiring only 3 floats per node while achieving significantly reduced dispersion (Table 2).
  2. The high-order scheme delivers the lowest overall dispersion at the cost of a stricter CFL condition \(\eqref{eq:ho-cfl}\) and wider stencil, making it ideal for long-range propagation.
  3. The DWM provides a physically intuitive formulation with guaranteed passivity, but has the highest memory cost (13 floats per node).
  4. The standard FDTD remains a strong general-purpose choice with the simplest implementation and competitive performance.
  5. Total simulation cost is comparable across schemes for equivalent accuracy targets, as higher per-step cost is offset by fewer steps or coarser grids (Section 10.2).
  6. Acoustic parameters (\(T_{60}\), EDT, \(C_{80}\), \(D_{50}\)) extracted from FDTD impulse responses agree with analytical predictions for simple geometries (Section 9).

Future work will extend the framework in several directions:

  • Pseudo-Spectral Time-Domain (PSTD) and k-space methods using FFT-based spatial derivatives [28].
  • Adaptive Rectangular Decomposition (ARD) for efficient large-domain simulation [29].
  • Perfectly Matched Layer (PML) absorbing boundaries [21] for improved open-boundary modeling.
  • Frequency-dependent impedance boundary conditions using digital filter design [18].
  • GPU acceleration via WebGPU compute shaders for real-time large-scale simulations.
  • Adaptive mesh refinement for geometrically complex domains.
  • Full acoustic parameter suite per ISO 3382-1 [17] including octave-band analysis.
  • Coupling with aeroacoustic models for flow-noise interaction.

The open-source nature of this project invites community contributions. All source code, documentation, benchmarks, and example scenarios are available at the project repository.
