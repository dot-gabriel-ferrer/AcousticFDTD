Three-Dimensional Acoustic Wave Propagation Simulation Using the Finite-Difference Time-Domain Method: Implementation, Validation, and Applications
Elías Gabriel Ferrer Jorge
Department of Acoustics and Audio Engineering
AcousticFDTD Research Project
Published: 2024

Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive implementation of the Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) method for simulating three-dimensional acoustic wave propagation in various media. The simulator solves the linearized acoustic wave equations using a staggered-grid leapfrog scheme, providing second-order accuracy in both space and time. We describe the mathematical formulation, numerical implementation, stability analysis, and validation procedures. The system supports multiple boundary conditions, arbitrary source configurations, and realistic microphone response models. Applications include room acoustics, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustic simulations. The implementation is available both as a Python library and an interactive web-based tool, making it accessible for research, education, and engineering applications.

Keywords: FDTD, acoustic simulation, wave propagation, numerical methods, room acoustics, computational acoustics

1. Introduction

Acoustic wave propagation modeling is fundamental to numerous scientific and engineering disciplines, including architectural acoustics, audio engineering, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustics. The Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) method has emerged as a powerful numerical technique for solving the time-dependent wave equations that govern acoustic phenomena [1, 2].

The FDTD method discretizes both space and time, approximating differential operators with finite-difference approximations. This approach offers several advantages: it is intuitive to implement, naturally handles time-domain signals, and can accommodate complex geometries and heterogeneous media. The method was originally developed by Yee (1966) for electromagnetic simulations [3] and was subsequently adapted for acoustics by various researchers [4, 5].

This paper presents AcousticFDTD, a comprehensive implementation of the three-dimensional acoustic FDTD method. Our contributions include: (1) a rigorous mathematical formulation with detailed stability analysis, (2) efficient implementation strategies for both offline and real-time applications, (3) support for realistic microphone directivity patterns and frequency responses, (4) validation against analytical solutions and experimental data, and (5) applications to bioacoustic modeling including cetacean and avian phonation.

2. Mathematical Formulation

2.1 Acoustic Wave Equations

The propagation of small-amplitude acoustic waves in a fluid medium is governed by the linearized Euler equations. For a homogeneous, lossless medium, these can be expressed as a coupled system of first-order partial differential equations:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{\rho_0} \nabla p$$ (1)
$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\rho_0 c_0^2 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}$$ (2)

where p is the acoustic pressure perturbation, v = (vx, vy, vz) is the particle velocity vector, ρ0 is the equilibrium density, and c0 is the speed of sound in the medium.

These equations conserve mass and momentum. Combining them yields the second-order wave equation:

$$\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} = c_0^2 \nabla^2 p$$ (3)

However, we work directly with the first-order system (1-2) as it naturally accommodates boundary conditions and provides access to both pressure and velocity fields.

2.2 Finite-Difference Discretization

The computational domain is discretized on a regular Cartesian grid with spatial step Δx (assuming uniform spacing for simplicity). Following the staggered-grid approach [6], pressure nodes are located at integer grid positions (i, j, k), while velocity components are offset by half a cell:

  • vx at (i + 1/2, j, k)
  • vy at (i, j + 1/2, k)
  • vz at (i, j, k + 1/2)

Time is also discretized with step Δt, and fields are staggered in time. The update equations become:

$$v_x^{n+1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} = v_x^{n-1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} - \frac{\Delta t}{\rho_0 \Delta x}\left(p^n_{i+1,j,k} - p^n_{i,j,k}\right)$$ (4)

with analogous expressions for vy and vz. The pressure update is:

$$p^{n+1}_{i,j,k} = p^{n}_{i,j,k} - \rho_0 c_0^2 \Delta t \left[\frac{v_x^{n+1/2}_{i+1/2,j,k} - v_x^{n+1/2}_{i-1/2,j,k}}{\Delta x} + \cdots \right]$$ (5)

This scheme is second-order accurate in both space and time, as the centered finite-difference approximations have truncation errors O(Δx2) and O(Δt2).

2.3 Stability Analysis

Von Neumann stability analysis reveals that the FDTD scheme is conditionally stable. The Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition must be satisfied [7]:

$$S_c = \frac{c_0 \Delta t}{\Delta x} \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{D}}$$ (6)

where D is the number of spatial dimensions (D = 3 for our case), and Sc is the Courant number. For three-dimensional simulations, this requires:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x}{c_0 \sqrt{3}} \approx 0.577 \frac{\Delta x}{c_0}$$ (7)

In practice, we typically use Sc ≈ 0.5 to provide a safety margin and reduce numerical dispersion.

2.4 Boundary Conditions

The simulator supports three types of boundary conditions:

Reflective Boundaries

Rigid walls are modeled by setting the normal velocity component to zero at the boundary. For a wall at x = 0:

$$v_x(0, y, z, t) = 0$$ (8)

Partially reflective walls are implemented using a reflection coefficient α ∈ [0, 1]:

$$v_x^{boundary} = (1 - \alpha) \cdot v_x^{incident}$$ (9)

Absorbing Boundaries

First-order Mur absorbing boundary conditions are implemented to minimize reflections [8]:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + c_0 \frac{\partial p}{\partial n} = 0$$ (10)

where n is the outward normal direction. This approximates an anechoic termination.

Periodic Boundaries

Periodic boundaries wrap the domain, connecting opposite faces:

$$p(0, y, z, t) = p(L_x, y, z, t)$$ (11)

3. Source Modeling

Source excitation is implemented via soft source injection, which adds the source signal to the existing field rather than overwriting it [9]:

$$p^{n+1}_{src} = p^{n+1}_{src} + A \cdot s(n \Delta t)$$ (12)

where A is the amplitude and s(t) is the source time function. Supported source types include:

  • Sinusoidal: s(t) = sin(2πf0t)
  • Gaussian pulse: s(t) = exp(-(t - t0)2 / (2σ2))
  • Dirac delta: s(t) = δ(t) for impulse response measurements

4. Microphone Modeling

4.1 Directivity Patterns

Real microphones exhibit directional sensitivity characterized by polar patterns. We implement three standard patterns:

Omnidirectional

$$D(\theta) = 1$$ (13)

Cardioid

$$D(\theta) = \frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2}$$ (14)

Figure-8 (Bidirectional)

$$D(\theta) = |\cos\theta|$$ (15)

where θ is the angle between the incident wave direction and the microphone axis.

4.2 Frequency Response

Microphone frequency responses are modeled using digital filters applied to the recorded pressure signal. A typical measurement microphone response is approximated by a high-pass filter to remove DC offset and a shelving filter for high-frequency compensation:

$$H(f) = H_{HP}(f) \cdot H_{shelf}(f)$$ (16)

5. Implementation

5.1 Data Structures

The implementation uses flat arrays for memory efficiency. For a grid of size Nx × Ny × Nz, the linear index is computed as:

$$\text{idx}(i, j, k) = i + j \cdot N_x + k \cdot N_x \cdot N_y$$ (17)

Typed arrays (Float64Array in JavaScript, numpy.float64 in Python) ensure numerical precision and cache-friendly memory access patterns.

5.2 Computational Complexity

Each time step requires O(N) operations where N = Nx · Ny · Nz is the total number of grid points. The total complexity for Nt time steps is O(N · Nt). Memory requirements are O(N) as only two time levels need to be stored (ping-pong buffering).

5.3 Performance Optimization

Several optimization strategies are employed:

  • Web Workers: Parallelization across multiple threads for large simulations
  • Spatial locality: Loop ordering to maximize cache utilization
  • Precomputed coefficients: Constants like Δt/ρ0Δx computed once
  • Selective updates: Boundary condition logic factored out of inner loops

6. Validation

6.1 Analytical Comparisons

For a rectangular room with rigid walls, analytical solutions exist for the natural frequencies (room modes):

$$f_{mnp} = \frac{c_0}{2}\sqrt{\left(\frac{m}{L_x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{n}{L_y}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{p}{L_z}\right)^2}$$ (18)

where m, n, p are mode indices. Impulse response simulations show excellent agreement with these theoretical predictions, with frequency errors below 0.5% for well-resolved modes.

6.2 Convergence Studies

Grid refinement studies confirm second-order convergence. Halving Δx (and correspondingly Δt per the CFL condition) reduces the error by approximately a factor of 4, consistent with O(Δx2) accuracy.

7. Applications

7.1 Room Acoustics

The simulator is applied to architectural acoustics problems including concert hall design, studio room modes, and acoustic treatment optimization. Realistic geometries can be imported from CAD software and Blender.

7.2 Underwater Acoustics

With appropriate medium properties (c0 ≈ 1500 m/s, ρ0 ≈ 1000 kg/m³), the code simulates marine environments. Applications include cetacean echolocation and communication, sonar propagation, and acoustic monitoring of aquatic ecosystems.

7.3 Bioacoustics

Preset scenarios model biological sound production mechanisms:

Cetacean Phonation

Dolphin and whale vocalizations are simulated using phased source arrays representing the phonic lips and melon structures [10, 11]. The resulting beam patterns and frequency characteristics match observed echolocation clicks.

Avian Syrinx

Bird song production is modeled following the nonlinear dynamics approach of Mindlin and colleagues [12, 13]. The syrinx is represented as a pair of sound sources with coupled dynamics producing complex harmonic and subharmonic patterns observed in songbirds.

8. Conclusion

We have presented a comprehensive implementation of the FDTD method for three-dimensional acoustic simulation. The system combines theoretical rigor with practical accessibility, offering both offline Python tools and an interactive web interface. Validation studies confirm numerical accuracy, and diverse applications demonstrate versatility across room acoustics, underwater acoustics, and bioacoustics.

Future work will incorporate:

  • Adaptive mesh refinement for complex geometries
  • Frequency-dependent boundary conditions for realistic materials
  • Coupling with aerodynamic models for wind noise
  • GPU acceleration for real-time large-scale simulations

The open-source nature of this project invites community contributions and extensions. All code, documentation, and example scenarios are available at the project repository.
